# The Accounting Interview Mega-Pack: A Progressive Study Manuscript of Commonly Asked Technical Questions With Model Answers

## TL;DR
- This is a comprehensive, progressively organized question bank of the most commonly recurring **technical** accounting interview questions (no behavioral/"tell me about yourself" content) for U.S. corporate/enterprise accounting roles — staff accountant through senior accountant and accounting-focused analyst.
- The questions are aggregated and deduplicated from the most authoritative interview-prep and accounting-education sources (Wall Street Prep, Corporate Finance Institute, AccountingCoach, Indeed, Wall Street Oasis, Robert Half, university career centers, and others); the highest-frequency questions — "walk me through the three statements," the accounting equation, debits/credits, accrual vs. cash, the "$10 depreciation" flow-through — appear in **Sections 1–2** because they are the ones interviewers ask most.
- Master Sections 1 and 2 cold (fundamentals and applied practice); Sections 3 and 4 (deferred taxes, leases, consolidation, ratios, valuation-adjacent concepts) differentiate senior candidates and master's-level hires. Light, conceptual regulatory coverage only.

## Key Findings
- **The single most universal question across every source is "Walk me through the three financial statements" and "How are they connected."** If you can answer this fluently plus its companion ("how does a $10 increase in depreciation flow through?"), you have covered the backbone of the technical interview.
- **Fundamentals dominate.** The accounting equation, debits and credits, accrual vs. cash, the matching and revenue-recognition principles, accruals vs. deferrals, AR vs. AP, deferred revenue, working capital, and depreciation appear in virtually every list. These are weighted most heavily here.
- **Answer structure matters as much as content.** For flow-through questions, always use a fixed order (Income Statement → Cash Flow Statement → Balance Sheet), state your tax-rate assumption, and confirm the balance sheet balances.
- **Regulatory detail is intentionally kept light.** Know conceptually what GAAP is, GAAP vs. IFRS at a high level, what an audit is, and that revenue recognition follows the five-step model codified in FASB ASC 606 — but you are not expected to recite codification numbers.

# SECTION 1 — FOUNDATIONAL / ENTRY-LEVEL (Staff Accountant, Bachelor's Level)
*The absolute fundamentals. These are the questions that prove you are a well-grounded accountant.*

**1. What is the basic accounting equation?**
Assets = Liabilities + Equity. A company's resources (assets) are funded either by what it owes to others (liabilities) or by the owners' residual claim (equity). It must always balance because every resource has a source of funding.

**2. Why does the balance sheet always balance?**
Because of double-entry accounting: every transaction has equal and offsetting debits and credits. Since assets are funded by either liabilities or equity, the two sides remain equal by construction.

**3. What are debits and credits?**
They are the two sides of every double-entry transaction. Debits are recorded on the left, credits on the right, and total debits must always equal total credits. Whether a debit or credit increases or decreases an account depends on the account type: assets and expenses increase with debits; liabilities, equity, and revenue increase with credits.

**4. Which accounts have a normal debit balance, and which have a normal credit balance?**
Assets and expenses carry normal debit balances. Liabilities, equity, and revenue carry normal credit balances. A common mnemonic is "DEALER": Debits increase Dividends, Expenses, Assets; Credits increase Liabilities, Equity, Revenue.

**5. What are the three main financial statements?**
The income statement, the balance sheet, and the cash flow statement. The income statement shows profitability over a period; the balance sheet shows financial position at a point in time; the cash flow statement shows cash inflows and outflows over a period.

**6. Walk me through the income statement.**
It shows profitability over a period of time. It starts with revenue, subtracts cost of goods sold to get gross profit, subtracts operating expenses (SG&A, R&D) to get operating income (EBIT), subtracts interest to get pre-tax income, and subtracts taxes to arrive at net income.

**7. Walk me through the balance sheet.**
It shows a company's financial position at a specific point in time: assets on one side; liabilities and shareholders' equity on the other. Assets are split into current (cash, receivables, inventory, prepaids) and non-current (PP&E, intangibles, goodwill). Liabilities split into current (payables, accrued expenses, short-term debt) and non-current (long-term debt, deferred taxes). Equity includes common stock, APIC, and retained earnings.

**8. Walk me through the cash flow statement.**
It starts with net income and reconciles to the net change in cash across three sections: cash flow from operations (add back non-cash items like D&A; adjust for changes in working capital), cash flow from investing (capital expenditures, acquisitions, divestitures), and cash flow from financing (debt and equity issuance/repayment, dividends).

**9. How are the three financial statements connected?**
Net income from the income statement flows to the top of the cash flow statement and into retained earnings on the balance sheet. Non-cash items (D&A) are added back on the cash flow statement and reduce the related assets on the balance sheet. The ending cash balance on the cash flow statement becomes the cash line on the balance sheet. Changes in working-capital balance-sheet accounts flow through operating cash flow.

**10. If you could only look at one financial statement to judge a company's health, which would you pick?**
The cash flow statement, because "cash is king" — it shows how much actual cash the business generates, which is harder to manipulate than accrual earnings. (Acknowledge that all three together give the complete picture.)

**11. What is the difference between cash-basis and accrual-basis accounting?**
Cash-basis records revenue and expenses when cash changes hands. Accrual-basis records revenue when it is earned and expenses when they are incurred, regardless of cash timing. Accrual gives a truer picture of economic performance and is required under GAAP for most companies.

**12. What is the matching principle?**
It dictates that expenses be recorded in the same period as the revenues they help generate, rather than when cash is paid. For example, COGS is recognized when the related sale is recorded, not when inventory was purchased.

**13. What is the revenue recognition principle?**
Revenue is recognized when it is earned and the performance obligation is satisfied — i.e., when control of the good or service transfers to the customer — not necessarily when cash is received.

**14. What is the difference between accounts receivable and accounts payable?**
Accounts receivable is money owed to the company by customers for goods/services already delivered — an asset. Accounts payable is money the company owes to suppliers for goods/services received — a liability.

**15. What is deferred (unearned) revenue?**
It is cash received from a customer before the good or service has been delivered. It is recorded as a liability because the company still owes the customer performance; it converts to revenue as the obligation is satisfied.

**16. What is the difference between deferred revenue and accounts receivable?**
Deferred revenue is cash received in advance for goods/services not yet provided (a liability). Accounts receivable is cash owed to the company for goods/services already provided (an asset). They are essentially opposites.

**17. What is an accrued expense?**
An expense that has been incurred but not yet paid or invoiced, recorded as a liability at period-end (e.g., wages, interest, utilities) to comply with the matching principle.

**18. What is the difference between an accrued expense and accounts payable?**
Both are current liabilities for costs already incurred but not yet paid. Accounts payable arises when a vendor invoice has been received for a documented, exact amount. An accrued expense is a cost incurred for which no invoice has yet been received, so it is recorded — often as an estimate — at period-end.

**19. What is a prepaid expense?**
A payment made in advance for goods or services to be received in the future. It is initially recorded as a current asset, then expensed gradually through adjusting entries as the benefit is consumed (e.g., prepaid insurance or rent).

**20. What is the difference between an accrual and a deferral?**
An accrual recognizes revenue or expense before cash moves (e.g., accrued revenue, accrued expense). A deferral recognizes revenue or expense after cash moves (e.g., deferred revenue, prepaid expense). Both are adjusting entries used to align reporting with the matching and revenue-recognition principles.

**21. What is working capital?**
Working capital = current assets − current liabilities. It measures short-term liquidity — the company's ability to cover near-term obligations with near-term assets.

**22. What does negative working capital mean?**
It can be a sign of efficiency in some industries (e.g., grocery and restaurants), where customers pay upfront and suppliers extend credit, so the company is effectively financed by its suppliers. In other industries, it may signal an inability to meet near-term obligations.

**23. What is the general ledger?**
The master record of all of a company's financial accounts and transactions. Subledgers (AR, AP, etc.) feed into it, and financial statements are produced from it.

**24. What is the difference between a journal and a ledger?**
A journal is the chronological record where transactions are first recorded (the book of original entry). A ledger organizes those transactions by account, summarizing activity and balances for each account.

**25. What is a trial balance?**
A list of all general-ledger accounts and their balances at a point in time, used to verify that total debits equal total credits before preparing financial statements.

**26. What is double-entry accounting?**
A system in which every transaction affects at least two accounts, with total debits equal to total credits. It keeps the accounting equation in balance and provides built-in error checking.

**27. What is a contra account? Give examples.**
A contra account carries a balance opposite to its paired account and reduces that account's reported value. Examples: Accumulated Depreciation (contra-asset offsetting PP&E), Allowance for Doubtful Accounts (contra-asset offsetting AR), and Treasury Stock (contra-equity) or Sales Returns and Allowances (contra-revenue).

**28. What is depreciation?**
The systematic allocation of the cost of a tangible long-lived asset over its useful life, matching the expense to the periods that benefit from the asset. It is a non-cash expense.

**29. What is the difference between depreciation and amortization?**
Depreciation applies to tangible assets (PP&E); amortization applies to intangible assets (patents, software, certain intangibles). Both spread cost over useful life.

**30. What are the main depreciation methods?**
Straight-line, declining balance (commonly double-declining), and units of production. Straight-line spreads cost evenly: (Cost − Salvage Value) / Useful Life. Double-declining balance is accelerated, applying twice the straight-line rate to beginning book value (salvage is not subtracted in the formula but acts as a floor). Units of production ties depreciation to actual output.

**31. When do you capitalize a cost versus expense it?**
If the purchase provides benefit beyond one year (used in ongoing operations), it is capitalized as an asset and depreciated/amortized. If it is consumed within the period, it is expensed immediately. Note: land is not depreciated.

**32. What is the accounting cycle?**
The sequence from recording transactions to producing statements: identify/record transactions in journals, post to the ledger, prepare an unadjusted trial balance, make adjusting entries, prepare an adjusted trial balance, produce financial statements, and make closing entries.

**33. What are adjusting entries?**
Entries made at period-end to record revenues earned and expenses incurred that have not yet been captured, so statements comply with accrual accounting. The three types are accruals, deferrals, and estimates (e.g., depreciation, bad debts).

**34. What are closing entries?**
Entries that zero out temporary accounts (revenues, expenses, dividends) at year-end and transfer their net effect to retained earnings, preparing the books for the new period. Permanent (balance-sheet) accounts are not closed.

**35. What is the difference between adjusting and closing entries?**
Adjusting entries (made before statements, often monthly) align records with accrual accounting. Closing entries (made after year-end statements) reset temporary accounts to zero for the next cycle.

**36. What is a T-account?**
A visual representation of a ledger account shaped like a "T," with debits on the left and credits on the right, used to analyze how entries affect an account's balance.

**37. What is GAAP?**
Generally Accepted Accounting Principles — the common set of U.S. accounting standards and rules that govern how financial statements are prepared, ensuring consistency, comparability, and transparency for users.

**38. What is the difference between GAAP and IFRS at a high level?**
GAAP (U.S.) is more rules-based; IFRS (international) is more principles-based. Notable conceptual differences: IFRS prohibits LIFO inventory; IFRS permits upward revaluation of certain assets and previously impaired assets, while GAAP generally does not.

**39. What is COGS?**
Cost of goods sold — the direct costs of producing the goods or delivering the services a company sells (direct materials, direct labor). It is subtracted from revenue to compute gross profit.

**40. What is the difference between COGS and operating expenses?**
COGS captures direct costs tied to production of goods/services. Operating expenses (SG&A, R&D) are indirect costs not directly tied to production. Both reduce income but appear on different lines.

**41. What is gross profit and gross margin?**
Gross profit = revenue − COGS. Gross margin = gross profit / revenue, expressed as a percentage. It measures profitability after direct costs.

**42. What is net income?**
The "bottom line" — profit remaining after all expenses, including COGS, operating expenses, interest, and taxes, are subtracted from revenue.

**43. What is retained earnings?**
The cumulative net income a company has kept (not paid out as dividends) since inception. It links the income statement to the balance sheet: ending RE = beginning RE + net income − dividends.

**44. What is a fixed asset?**
A long-lived tangible asset (PP&E — property, plant, and equipment) used in operations to generate revenue over more than one year, not held for resale.

**45. What is inventory?**
Goods a company holds for sale (or raw materials/work-in-process used to produce them). It is a current asset and becomes COGS when sold.

# SECTION 2 — INTERMEDIATE (Staff-to-Senior Accountant; Applied Practice)
*Journal entries, statement interactions, and the common scenario questions that show you can actually do the work.*

**46. How does a $10 increase in depreciation flow through the three statements? (assume a 20% tax rate)**
Income statement: depreciation rises $10, reducing pre-tax income by $10 and net income by $8 (at 20% tax). Cash flow statement: start with net income down $8, add back the $10 non-cash depreciation, so cash from operations rises $2; ending cash up $2. Balance sheet: cash up $2, PP&E down $10, so assets down $8; on the other side retained earnings down $8 from lower net income — so it balances. (Always state your tax-rate assumption and confirm the balance sheet balances.)

**47. What immediately happens on the income statement if inventory goes up by $10?**
Nothing — it is a trick question. Buying inventory is an asset swap (or creates a payable); the income statement is only affected later when the inventory is sold and becomes COGS. The immediate impact is on the balance sheet and cash flow statement.

**48. How does an inventory write-down affect the three statements?**
Balance sheet: inventory (asset) falls by the write-down and equity falls. Income statement: an expense (in COGS or a separate line) reduces net income. Cash flow statement: the write-down is added back to operating cash flow as a non-cash expense, but you must avoid double-counting it in working-capital changes.

**49. What are the journal entries when a company sells inventory on credit under a perpetual system?**
Two entries. First, record the revenue: debit Accounts Receivable, credit Sales (at selling price). Second, record the cost and reduce inventory: debit Cost of Goods Sold, credit Inventory (at the company's cost).

**50. What is the journal entry to record a credit sale, then collection?**
At sale: debit Accounts Receivable, credit Revenue. At collection: debit Cash, credit Accounts Receivable (the asset converts from a receivable to cash; revenue is not touched again).

**51. What is the journal entry when a customer prepays for a service?**
Debit Cash, credit Deferred (Unearned) Revenue — a liability. As the service is delivered, debit Deferred Revenue and credit Revenue.

**52. What is the journal entry for accruing wages not yet paid?**
Debit Wages Expense, credit Wages Payable at period-end. When paid, debit Wages Payable and credit Cash.

**53. What is the journal entry for prepaid insurance and its adjustment?**
At payment: debit Prepaid Insurance (asset), credit Cash. Each period as it expires: debit Insurance Expense, credit Prepaid Insurance for the portion used.

**54. What is the allowance method for bad debts, and what is the entry?**
The allowance method estimates uncollectible receivables in advance to satisfy the matching principle. The entry: debit Bad Debt Expense, credit Allowance for Doubtful Accounts (a contra-asset). When a specific account is written off: debit Allowance for Doubtful Accounts, credit Accounts Receivable.

**55. How does the allowance method differ from the direct write-off method?**
The allowance method estimates and records bad debt expense in the same period as the related sales (GAAP-compliant, satisfies matching). The direct write-off method records bad debt only when a specific account is deemed uncollectible — simpler but violates matching and is not GAAP-compliant for reporting.

**56. What is the bank reconciliation process?**
It compares the company's book cash balance to the bank statement balance to identify and resolve differences. On the bank side, add deposits in transit and subtract outstanding checks; on the book side, adjust for bank fees, NSF checks, interest earned, and bank-collected items. Record journal entries only for the book-side items, and confirm the adjusted balances agree.

**57. What are common reconciling items in a bank reconciliation?**
Outstanding checks (issued but not yet cleared), deposits in transit (recorded by the company but not yet by the bank), bank service fees, NSF (bounced) checks, interest earned, and errors by either party.

**58. What is the month-end close process?**
The set of procedures to finalize the books each month: reconcile all accounts, record accruals and deferrals, post adjusting/recurring journal entries, review variances, and prepare financial statements. The goal is accurate, timely reporting; strong candidates mention checklists, cutoffs, ownership, and review steps.

**59. What is a reconciliation, and why is it important?**
Comparing two sets of records (e.g., the GL balance to a bank statement or subledger) to ensure they agree, investigating and resolving differences. It is a key control that catches errors, omissions, and potential fraud before sign-off.

**60. What are the inventory costing methods — FIFO, LIFO, and weighted average?**
FIFO (first-in, first-out) assumes the oldest costs go to COGS first. LIFO (last-in, first-out) assumes the newest costs go to COGS first. Weighted average uses a single average unit cost. The physical flow need not match the cost-flow assumption.

**61. In a period of rising prices, how do FIFO and LIFO differ in their effects?**
With rising prices, FIFO produces lower COGS, higher net income, and a higher ending inventory balance. LIFO produces higher COGS, lower net income, lower taxable income (a tax advantage), and a lower/understated inventory balance. Weighted average falls between the two.

**62. Is LIFO allowed under IFRS?**
No. LIFO is permitted under U.S. GAAP but prohibited under IFRS. In the U.S., the LIFO conformity rule requires a company using LIFO for taxes to also use it for financial reporting.

**63. What is goodwill and how is it created?**
Goodwill is an intangible asset created when a company acquires another business for more than the fair value of its net identifiable assets. It represents items like brand, customer relationships, and synergies that cannot be separately identified. It increases only through acquisitions.

**64. Is goodwill amortized?**
Under U.S. GAAP (FASB ASC 350, Intangibles—Goodwill and Other), goodwill is not amortized; it is tested for impairment at least annually and written down if impaired. (Note: private companies have an option to amortize goodwill, created by FASB ASU 2014-02, and IFRS likewise requires impairment testing rather than amortization.)

**65. What is goodwill impairment?**
When the carrying value of goodwill exceeds its recoverable/fair value, the company writes it down, recording an impairment loss on the income statement. The write-down is a non-cash charge.

**66. What is EBITDA?**
Earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization. It is a proxy for core operating cash flow before capital structure and tax effects, calculated as EBIT + D&A. It is widely used for comparing companies with different capital structures.

**67. Why might EBITDA not be a good measure of cash flow?**
Because it ignores changes in working capital, excludes capital expenditures (which can be substantial), and adds back D&A while interest and taxes are real cash costs. It can therefore be far from true cash flow and can be manipulated. Warren Buffett is famously critical of it — in Berkshire Hathaway's 2000 Chairman's letter he wrote, "References to EBITDA make us shudder — does management think the tooth fairy pays for capital expenditures?" — emphasizing that depreciation reflects a real, cash-funded cost of replacing assets.

**68. What is the difference between EBIT and EBITDA?**
EBIT (operating income) is profitability after depreciation and amortization but before interest and taxes. EBITDA adds D&A back to EBIT, removing the effect of non-cash asset charges and getting closer to operating cash flow.

**69. What is the difference between net income and cash flow?**
Net income is an accrual-based accounting measure that includes non-cash items (depreciation, accruals) and excludes some cash flows (capex, debt principal). Cash flow tracks actual cash movement. A profitable company can still run out of cash, and vice versa.

**70. What is free cash flow?**
Cash flow available after the company funds the investments needed to maintain operations. A common definition: operating cash flow − capital expenditures. It represents cash "free" for debt repayment, dividends, or reinvestment.

**71. What is the difference between cost accounting, financial accounting, and managerial accounting?**
Financial accounting produces external statements under GAAP for investors and creditors. Managerial accounting produces internal information for planning, control, and decision-making (not GAAP-bound). Cost accounting, a bridge between them, determines and controls the cost of products and services.

**72. What is the difference between fixed and variable costs?**
Fixed costs stay constant within a relevant range regardless of output (e.g., rent, salaries). Variable costs change in proportion to production volume (e.g., raw materials, direct labor). Mixed/semi-variable costs have both components.

**73. What is the difference between direct and indirect costs?**
Direct costs can be traced to a specific product, project, or cost object (raw materials, direct labor). Indirect costs (overhead) support operations broadly and cannot be traced directly (factory utilities, administrative salaries).

**74. What is break-even analysis?**
It identifies the point where total revenue equals total cost, so there is neither profit nor loss. Break-even units = fixed costs / contribution margin per unit (price − variable cost per unit).

**75. What is contribution margin?**
Selling price per unit minus variable cost per unit (or, in total, revenue minus variable costs). It represents the amount available to cover fixed costs and then contribute to profit.

**76. What is a sunk cost?**
A cost already incurred that cannot be recovered and therefore should not influence future decisions (e.g., the depreciated book value of an old machine when deciding on replacement).

**77. What is the difference between a cost center and a profit center?**
A cost center incurs costs but does not directly generate revenue (e.g., accounting department). A profit center is responsible for both revenues and costs and is measured on its profitability.

**78. How does buying $100 of equipment with cash affect the three statements at the moment of purchase?**
Income statement: no immediate impact (capex is not an expense; depreciation comes later). Cash flow statement: −$100 in investing activities. Balance sheet: cash down $100, PP&E up $100 — assets stay the same and it balances.

**79. What happens to the three statements when a company raises $100 of debt?**
Income statement: no immediate impact (interest comes later). Cash flow statement: +$100 in financing. Balance sheet: cash up $100, debt (liability) up $100 — balances.

**80. What is the difference between capital expenditures and operating expenses?**
Capital expenditures (capex) are purchases of long-lived assets, capitalized on the balance sheet and depreciated over time. Operating expenses are period costs fully expensed on the income statement when incurred.

**81. What is an audit?**
An independent examination of a company's financial statements and underlying records to provide assurance that they are fairly presented and in accordance with the applicable framework (e.g., GAAP). External audits are conducted by independent CPAs; internal audits review a company's own controls and processes.

**82. What is the difference between an internal and external audit?**
An internal audit is performed by the company's own staff to review controls, risk, and process efficiency. An external audit is conducted by an independent firm to express an opinion on the fairness of the financial statements for outside users.

# SECTION 3 — ADVANCED (Senior Accountant / Accounting-Focused Analyst)
*More complex concepts and analysis that distinguish experienced candidates.*

**83. What is the five-step revenue recognition model?**
(1) Identify the contract with the customer; (2) identify the performance obligations; (3) determine the transaction price; (4) allocate the transaction price to the obligations; (5) recognize revenue as each obligation is satisfied (at a point in time or over time as control transfers). This is the unified standard for revenue from contracts with customers, codified in FASB ASC 606 (issued as ASU 2014-09 in May 2014, with IFRS 15 as the international equivalent). Its core principle: an entity recognizes revenue to depict the transfer of promised goods or services in an amount reflecting the consideration it expects to be entitled to.

**84. How would you recognize revenue for an annual software subscription paid upfront?**
Record the cash receipt as deferred revenue (a liability), then recognize revenue ratably (typically straight-line) over the subscription period as the performance obligation is satisfied — e.g., 1/12 each month for an annual prepaid contract.

**85. What is a deferred tax liability and how is it created?**
A DTL arises from a temporary difference where book income exceeds taxable income now, so the company pays less cash tax today but will pay more later. The classic cause is using accelerated depreciation for tax but straight-line for books — lower taxable income early, higher later. It sits on the balance sheet as the future tax owed when the difference reverses.

**86. What is a deferred tax asset?**
A DTA arises when the company pays more cash tax now than its book tax expense implies, creating a future tax benefit. Common causes are net operating loss carryforwards and temporary differences (e.g., warranty or bad-debt reserves recognized for books before they are deductible for tax).

**87. What is the difference between a temporary and a permanent tax difference?**
A temporary difference reverses over time and creates a deferred tax asset or liability (e.g., depreciation timing). A permanent difference never reverses (e.g., tax-exempt municipal interest, certain fines), so it does not create a deferred tax item — it only changes the effective tax rate in the current period.

**88. What is the difference between an operating lease and a finance (capital) lease?**
A finance lease transfers substantially all the risks and rewards of ownership; the lessee records a right-of-use asset and a lease liability, and recognizes both amortization and interest expense (front-loaded total expense). An operating lease conveys the right to use without ownership; under current U.S. GAAP (FASB ASC 842, which replaced ASC 840) it is also on the balance sheet as a right-of-use asset and liability, but expense is recognized on a straight-line basis as a single lease expense.

**89. How are leases recorded on the balance sheet today?**
Under current standards (FASB ASC 842, effective for public companies in 2019 and private companies in 2022), essentially all leases longer than 12 months are capitalized: the lessee records a right-of-use asset and a corresponding lease liability equal to the present value of future lease payments. This ended most "off-balance-sheet" operating-lease treatment; short-term leases of 12 months or less may be elected out of recognition.

**90. Does lease accounting change a company's actual cash flow?**
No. Regardless of how a lease is classified or presented, the company pays the same cash lease payments. Classification affects the geography and timing of expense recognition on the statements, not total cash paid.

**91. What is consolidation, and when is it required?**
When a parent controls another entity (generally >50% ownership), it must fully consolidate — combining 100% of the subsidiary's assets, liabilities, revenues, and expenses with its own, then eliminating intercompany transactions.

**92. What is noncontrolling (minority) interest?**
When a parent owns more than 50% but less than 100% of a subsidiary, it still consolidates 100% of the subsidiary's financials. The portion of the subsidiary's equity (and net income) not owned by the parent is reported as noncontrolling interest, shown within equity on the consolidated balance sheet.

**93. What is the equity method of accounting?**
Used when an investor has significant influence but not control (typically 20–50% ownership). The investment is recorded at cost and adjusted for the investor's proportionate share of the investee's net income (increasing the investment and recognizing income) and dividends (reducing the investment).

**94. How do you build a cash flow statement from the income statement and balance sheet?**
Start with net income. Add back non-cash items (D&A, stock-based comp, impairments). Adjust for changes in working-capital accounts (e.g., an increase in AR or inventory is a use of cash; an increase in AP is a source). Add investing items (capex, acquisitions) and financing items (debt/equity issuance and repayment, dividends). The net change in cash should tie to the change in the balance-sheet cash line.

**95. Why does an increase in accounts receivable reduce cash flow?**
Because revenue was recorded (boosting net income) but cash has not yet been collected. The increase in AR is a use of cash, subtracted in the operating section to reconcile accrual net income to actual cash generated.

**96. Why does an increase in accounts payable increase cash flow?**
Because the company recorded an expense or purchase but has not yet paid cash. Deferring that payment conserves cash, so the increase in AP is a source of cash in the operating section.

**97. What is the current ratio?**
Current assets / current liabilities. It measures short-term liquidity — the ability to cover obligations due within a year. A ratio above 1.0 means current assets exceed current liabilities.

**98. What is the quick (acid-test) ratio?**
(Current assets − inventory) / current liabilities, or (cash + marketable securities + receivables) / current liabilities. It is a stricter liquidity measure that excludes inventory because inventory can be slow to convert to cash.

**99. What is days sales outstanding (DSO)?**
The average number of days it takes to collect receivables: (Accounts Receivable / Revenue) × number of days in the period. A lower DSO indicates faster collections and stronger cash flow.

**100. What is inventory turnover?**
COGS / average inventory. It measures how many times inventory is sold and replaced in a period. Higher turnover generally indicates efficient inventory management; days inventory outstanding = 365 / inventory turnover.

**101. What is the cash conversion cycle?**
Days inventory outstanding + days sales outstanding − days payable outstanding. It measures how long cash is tied up in operations from paying suppliers to collecting from customers. A shorter cycle is generally better.

**102. What is return on equity (ROE)?**
Net income / shareholders' equity. It measures how efficiently a company generates profit from shareholders' invested capital.

**103. What is return on assets (ROA)?**
Net income / total assets. It measures how efficiently a company uses its asset base to generate profit.

**104. What is the debt-to-equity ratio?**
Total debt / shareholders' equity. It is a leverage ratio showing how much a company relies on debt versus equity financing; higher ratios indicate greater financial risk.

**105. What is the difference between operating, investing, and financing cash flows?**
Operating cash flows come from core business activities (net income plus non-cash adjustments and working-capital changes). Investing cash flows relate to long-term assets (capex, acquisitions, asset sales). Financing cash flows relate to raising/returning capital (debt, equity, dividends).

**106. How do you record PP&E and what events affect it?**
There are four key events: initial purchase (capitalized at cost), depreciation (allocated over useful life), additions/capital expenditures (increase the balance), and dispositions (remove the asset, recognize gain/loss). PP&E is often the main revenue-generating capital asset.

**107. How does a sale of a fixed asset affect the statements?**
Income statement: a gain or loss equal to proceeds minus net book value. Cash flow statement: full cash proceeds appear in investing activities, and the non-cash gain/loss is reversed out of operating activities. Balance sheet: the asset (and its accumulated depreciation) is removed, and cash increases by the proceeds.

**108. What is a bond issued at a discount versus a premium?**
A bond is issued at a discount when its coupon (stated) rate is below the market rate, so investors pay less than face value. It is issued at a premium when the coupon rate is above the market rate, so investors pay more than face value. The discount/premium is amortized over the bond's life.

**109. What does the effective interest method do?**
It amortizes a bond's discount or premium by applying the market rate at issuance to the bond's carrying value each period to compute interest expense. The difference between that expense and the cash coupon is the amortization, which brings the carrying value to face value by maturity. It is the GAAP-preferred method.

**110. What are the most common profitability margins?**
Gross margin (gross profit / revenue), operating margin (operating income / revenue), EBITDA margin (EBITDA / revenue), and net profit margin (net income / revenue). Each strips out progressively more costs to measure profitability at different levels.

**111. How do you estimate a company's bad debt expense?**
Common approaches: percentage-of-sales (apply a historical bad-debt rate to credit sales) or aging-of-receivables (apply increasing loss rates to older receivable buckets to set the required allowance balance). Both rely on historical experience and judgment.

**112. What is a budget, and what are common budgeting methods?**
A budget is a financial plan estimating revenues and expenses for a future period. Common methods include incremental budgeting (adjusting the prior period), zero-based budgeting (justifying every cost from zero each cycle), and value/activity-based budgeting.

**113. What is variance analysis?**
Comparing actual results to budgeted or standard amounts to identify and explain differences (favorable or unfavorable), then investigating causes to inform corrective action — a core management-accounting control.

# SECTION 4 — MOST ADVANCED / MASTER'S-LEVEL (Conceptual Mastery, Theory, and Valuation-Adjacent Concepts)
*Sophisticated technical, theoretical, and analytical questions.*

**114. Walk me through a multi-step scenario: a company buys a $100 factory funded entirely with debt. Year 1, then Year 2 with 10% interest and 10% straight-line depreciation. How do the statements move?**
Year 1 at purchase: no income-statement impact; cash flow shows −$100 investing and +$100 financing; balance sheet shows PP&E +$100 and debt +$100. Year 2: depreciation of $10 and interest of $10 reduce pre-tax income by $20; at a 40% tax rate net income falls $12. Cash flow: net income −$12, add back $10 depreciation → operating cash flow −$2 (interest is in net income). Balance sheet: PP&E down $10 (now $90), cash down by the period's net cash change, retained earnings down $12 — keep tracing each line and confirm it balances. (The key skill is a consistent order and a balanced result.)

**115. If a company writes down PP&E by $100 (impairment), how do the three statements respond? (40% tax)**
Income statement: $100 impairment expense reduces pre-tax income by $100 and net income by $60. Cash flow: net income −$60, add back the $100 non-cash impairment → operating cash flow +$40 (from the tax savings). Balance sheet: PP&E down $100, cash up $40 → assets down $60; retained earnings down $60 — balances.

**116. Why can a profitable company go bankrupt?**
Because profit (accrual net income) is not cash. A company can recognize revenue on credit sales, tie up cash in inventory and receivables, carry heavy capex or debt-principal obligations, and run out of liquidity even while reporting profits. This is why the cash flow statement and working-capital management are critical.

**117. What is the difference between enterprise value and equity value?**
Equity value (market cap) is the value attributable to shareholders. Enterprise value is the value of the entire business to all capital providers: equity value + debt + minority interest + preferred − cash and equivalents. Metrics with interest deducted (net income, EPS) pair with equity value; metrics before interest (EBIT, EBITDA, revenue) pair with enterprise value.

**118. Why do you add noncontrolling interest when bridging from equity value to enterprise value?**
Because consolidated metrics like EBITDA include 100% of a partially owned subsidiary's financials, the enterprise value must also reflect 100% of that business. Adding back noncontrolling interest avoids a mismatch between the numerator (value) and denominator (a metric that includes the full subsidiary).

**119. How does a deferred tax liability arise in an acquisition (asset write-up)?**
When acquired assets are written up to fair value for book purposes but not for tax, the higher book basis produces book depreciation/amortization that is not deductible for tax. That future non-deductible expense creates a DTL, recorded through goodwill in purchase accounting, which reverses (declines) as the extra book depreciation is recognized over time.

**120. Conceptually, how does deferred tax accounting uphold the matching principle?**
By recording tax expense based on the book income reported in a period — not just the cash tax paid — deferred tax accounting matches the tax consequences of transactions to the period in which the underlying income or expense is recognized, smoothing the relationship between pre-tax book income and tax expense.

**121. What makes a good three-statement financial model?**
It is clearly laid out, identifies the key business drivers, is internally consistent (the three statements fully link and the balance sheet balances), uses transparent and clearly flagged assumptions, includes error checks, and can flex under different scenarios without breaking. Working-capital items are typically driven by ratios like receivable days and inventory days tied to revenue and COGS.

**122. How do you forecast working-capital items in a model?**
Tie them to operating drivers using "days" or "turns" ratios: forecast accounts receivable from DSO and revenue, inventory from inventory days and COGS, and accounts payable from payable days and COGS/purchases. The period-over-period changes flow into operating cash flow.

**123. What is the conceptual difference between book value and market value?**
Book value is the accounting carrying amount on the balance sheet (historical cost less depreciation/impairment, adjusted for equity). Market value reflects what investors will pay today, incorporating future expectations, intangibles, and growth. The two diverge because accounting is largely historical-cost-based and conservative.

**124. Can a company have negative shareholders' equity? What does it mean?**
Yes. It occurs when liabilities exceed assets — usually from accumulated losses, large debt-funded payouts, or share buybacks exceeding retained earnings. It is not always fatal (a highly profitable company that returns large amounts of capital can show it), but it can also signal financial distress.

**125. What are the qualitative characteristics of useful financial information?**
Under the conceptual framework, the fundamental characteristics are relevance (capable of influencing decisions) and faithful representation (complete, neutral, free from error). Enhancing characteristics are comparability, verifiability, timeliness, and understandability.

**126. What is the principle of conservatism (prudence)?**
The convention that, when uncertain, accountants should avoid overstating assets/income and understating liabilities/expenses — e.g., recognizing probable losses promptly (inventory write-downs, bad-debt allowances) but not anticipating gains. It biases toward caution to avoid misleading users.

**127. What is the going-concern assumption?**
The assumption that a company will continue operating for the foreseeable future, which justifies recording assets at cost (rather than liquidation value) and deferring certain costs. If going concern is in doubt, it must be disclosed and may change how items are valued.

**128. What is "substance over form"?**
The principle that transactions should be accounted for based on their economic reality rather than merely their legal form — for example, capitalizing a lease that effectively transfers ownership risks and rewards even if legal title does not pass.

**129. What is the difference between an accretive and dilutive acquisition (conceptually)?**
An acquisition is accretive if it increases the acquirer's earnings per share and dilutive if it decreases EPS, after accounting for the cost of financing (debt interest or new shares) versus the additional earnings acquired. It is a quick gauge of an all-else-equal earnings impact, not of value creation.

**130. How does stock-based compensation flow through the statements?**
Income statement: it is a non-cash operating expense reducing pre-tax income and net income. Cash flow: it is added back to operating cash flow because no cash leaves the business. Balance sheet: it increases equity (paid-in capital), offsetting the reduction in retained earnings from lower net income.

**131. What is the difference between basic and diluted EPS?**
Basic EPS = net income available to common / weighted-average common shares. Diluted EPS additionally assumes the conversion of dilutive securities (options, convertibles, warrants), increasing the share count to show the most conservative per-share figure.

**132. How would you analyze whether a company's earnings are high quality?**
Compare net income to operating cash flow over time (large, persistent gaps can signal aggressive accruals), examine reliance on one-time/non-recurring items, scrutinize revenue-recognition aggressiveness and changes in working capital, and assess whether earnings convert into cash. High-quality earnings are cash-backed, recurring, and conservatively recognized.

**133. What is comprehensive income?**
Net income plus other comprehensive income (OCI) — items that bypass the income statement, such as certain foreign-currency translation adjustments, some pension adjustments, and unrealized gains/losses on certain securities. It captures the total change in equity from non-owner sources.

**134. What is the difference between a write-down and a write-off?**
A write-down reduces an asset's carrying value to a lower recoverable amount (partial reduction). A write-off removes the asset entirely (reduces it to zero) when it has no remaining recoverable value, such as an uncollectible receivable.

## Recommendations
**Stage 1 — Lock in the fundamentals (Section 1 and the high-frequency items in Section 2).** Be able to recite, cold and conversationally: the accounting equation, debits/credits and normal balances, the three statements and their linkages, accrual vs. cash, matching and revenue recognition, AR/AP, deferred revenue, accruals vs. deferrals, working capital, and depreciation. Benchmark: you can answer "walk me through the three statements" and "how are they connected" in under 90 seconds each without hesitation.

**Stage 2 — Drill the flow-through and journal-entry mechanics (Section 2).** Practice the "$10 depreciation" question and its variants (inventory write-down, capex, asset sale, impairment) until you can do them in a fixed order (IS → CFS → BS), always stating the tax rate and confirming the balance sheet balances. Benchmark: you can handle any single-item flow-through question in 30–60 seconds and a multi-step scenario in 1–2 minutes.

**Stage 3 — Layer in advanced and theoretical depth (Sections 3–4) based on the target role.** For a senior accountant or accounting-analyst role, prioritize deferred taxes, leases, consolidation/NCI, the five-step revenue model, cash-flow construction, and ratio analysis. For master's-level or analyst-track roles, add enterprise vs. equity value, earnings quality, model logic, and the conceptual framework. Benchmark: you can explain a deferred tax liability and a finance-vs-operating lease distinction clearly to a non-expert.

**Cross-cutting tips:** State assumptions out loud; if no tax rate is given, ask. Lead with the high-level concept in plain English, then add technical depth. For any flow-through question, use one consistent order every time. Keep regulatory answers conceptual — know what GAAP is, GAAP vs. IFRS at a high level, and what an audit is, but do not volunteer codification numbers you cannot support.

## Caveats
- **Frequency is inferred, not measured.** "Most commonly asked" reflects strong convergence across many reputable prep and education sources, not a statistical survey. Exact questions vary by company, interviewer, and role.
- **Some sources blend corporate-accounting and investment-banking/finance interview content.** Flow-through, EBITDA, enterprise-value, and valuation-adjacent questions are more common for analyst-track and finance-leaning roles; pure staff-accountant interviews lean more on close, reconciliations, journal entries, and GAAP fundamentals. Sections are ordered to reflect this.
- **Standards evolve and have nuances.** Lease (ASC 842), revenue (ASC 606), and goodwill (ASC 350) rules carry private-company alternatives and GAAP/IFRS differences; answers here are intentionally high-level and conceptual per the brief. Verify specifics against current standards for technical roles.
- **Model answers are study scaffolding, not scripts.** Interviewers value genuine understanding and clear communication over memorized recitations; adapt the phrasing to your own voice and the specific role.
