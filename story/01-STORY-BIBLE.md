# 01 — THE STORY BIBLE

*The single creative source of truth. Every chapter sub-agent reads this first.
If the outline and the bible ever disagree, the bible wins on voice/world/cast;
NUMBERS.md wins on figures.*

---

## 0. Logline

> A newborn transaction named **Sal** must journey across the Accrual World — past
> strict gatekeepers, through a revenue labyrinth that splits Sal in two, surviving
> aging, audits, and a brush with death at year-end — to achieve the only thing a
> transaction ever wants: **to be recognized**, and printed forever into the
> company's public record.

## 1. Premise & the two layers

The book runs on **two interlocking layers** that mirror each other beat for beat:

1. **The Accrual World (the myth).** A living realm where every financial concept
   is a citizen, a place, or a force. This is where the fun, the stakes, and the
   characters live. Sal is the hero here.
2. **The Office (the practice).** Summit's actual accounting department, where a
   terrified-but-curious rookie, **Remy**, does the *real* bookkeeping that
   corresponds to whatever Sal is experiencing in the myth — the actual journal
   entries, the actual numbers from `NUMBERS.md`.

Every chapter weaves both: the mythic adventure carries the emotion and the
metaphor; the Office grounds it in real debits and credits the reader could
reproduce on paper. The Office is also where **interview questions** surface
naturally (see §7).

**Rule of the two layers:** the myth must never *contradict* the mechanics. If
the metaphor would teach something false, change the metaphor, not the
accounting. Accuracy is sacred; whimsy serves it.

## 2. Voice & tone (the style guide — READ THIS)

- **Reading level:** a bright 16-year-old or a first-year college student. Smart,
  never dumbed-down, never jargon-walled. Define every term the first time, in
  plain language, *then* use the real term proudly.
- **Comic voice:** warm, fast, a little mischievous — the lovechild of **Terry
  Pratchett, Douglas Adams, and a really good explainer (think *The Phantom
  Tollbooth* meets a great econ teacher)**. Jokes are frequent but never at the
  expense of clarity. The humor comes from *personifying dry things* and from the
  narrator's dry asides — not from random gags.
- **Sentences:** mostly short. Vary rhythm. Use the occasional one-line
  paragraph for a punchline or a key idea. Let big ideas land on their own line.
- **Second person, lightly.** The narrator can occasionally address "you," the
  reader, like a co-conspirator, especially when handing over a number to follow.
- **Numbers are characters too.** When a figure appears, treat it with respect:
  show where it came from and where it's going. Never drop a number without a
  lineage.
- **No condescension, no fear-mongering.** Accounting is presented as elegant and
  knowable, not scary. The villain is confusion, and we're beating it together.
- **Each chapter must teach truthfully enough that a reader could answer the real
  exam/interview question afterward.** Fun is the delivery; rigor is the payload.

**Things to avoid:** corporate-speak, dumping standard citations like footnotes
(weave "ASC 606" in as a named beast, not a citation), walls of definitions,
humor that mocks the learner, and metaphors that drift from the real mechanics.

## 3. Recurring structural devices (use in every chapter)

Each chapter should contain, in roughly this order:

1. **Cold open** — a vivid scene in the Accrual World (the "hook").
2. **The lesson, lived** — the concept taught *through* what happens to a
   character, not lectured. The metaphor carries the mechanics.
3. **Back at the Office** — a short cut to Remy doing the real entry/number, with
   the actual debits and credits from `NUMBERS.md`.
4. **Iris's Question** — controller Iris quizzes Remy (this is the interview
   question, in disguise); the answer is delivered in-scene. (See §7.)
5. **The Friar's Proverb** — a one-line piece of accounting wisdom from the
   founder-sage that crystallizes the chapter (a memory hook).
6. **Cliffhook** — a sentence that pulls toward the next station.

These are guidelines, not a rigid template — vary them so chapters don't feel
formulaic. But every chapter must hit the *lesson + real entry + interview beat*.

## 4. The world: the Accrual World and its stations

The Accrual World is the realm a transaction travels through between being *born*
(an economic event) and being *remembered* (a number in the filing). It is shaped
like a one-way road with named stations — the same order as the accounting cycle.

| Station (place) | Concept it embodies | Chapter |
|---|---|---|
| **The Genesis** (the loading dock / the signing room) | A transaction is born; A = L + E | 1 |
| **The War Room** | Managerial accounting — what to make, what it costs, the price tag | 2 |
| **The Gate of the Three-Way Match** | Source documents & internal controls | 3 |
| **The Labyrinth of Revenue** | ASC 606 five-step model; the three states of matter | 4 |
| **The Hall of Inventory** | FIFO vs LIFO; COGS | 5 |
| **The Matching Forge** | Matching principle; capitalize vs expense; amortization | 6 |
| **The Great Library** | Journalizing, ledgers, the trial balance | 7 |
| **The Workshop of Adjustments** | Accruals, deferrals, the 2×2 matrix; bank rec; close | 8 |
| **The Aging Room & the Oracle** | Depreciation; bad debt / CECL | 9 |
| **The Trinity Hall** | Income statement, balance sheet, cash flow | 10 |
| **The Reading Room** | Ratios, working capital, GAAP vs IFRS, leases, deferred taxes | 11 |
| **The Vault of Mergers** | Business combinations, consolidation, goodwill, impairment | 12 |
| **The Great Cleansing** | Closing & reversing entries; permanent vs temporary | 13 |
| **The Crucible** | The independent audit (PCAOB) | 14 |
| **The Public Stage** | The 10-K, the earnings call, Non-GAAP; immortality | 15 |

**Geography rule:** the road is one-way and chronological. A transaction cannot
skip a station. Once Sal passes a gate, that gate is behind them (callbacks are
allowed — "remember the Gate?" — but no retreading).

## 5. The cast

### Protagonist
- **Sal** *(the Sale)* — the hero. A newborn transaction: Summit's first big
  enterprise deal, the $90,000 TrailHead Pro bundle to Atlas Corp. Eager, naive,
  big-hearted, desperate to *matter*. Sal's one dream: to be **recognized** —
  literally (revenue recognition) and emotionally (to be seen, counted,
  remembered). Sal is a **temporary citizen** (a revenue), which means Sal is
  fated to "die" at year-end closing — the engine of the emotional arc.
  - **Lyle** *(the License, $72,000)* — Sal's "instant" half. Confident, quick,
    recognized *at a point in time* the moment the download key is handed over.
    A bit of a show-off: he arrives and is immediately Somebody.
  - **Sunny** *(the Support, $18,000)* — Sal's "patient" half. Recognized *over
    time*, $500 a month for 36 months. Born first as a **liability** (Deferred
    Revenue) — so Sunny starts life owing the world, and must *earn* her way into
    revenue, one month at a time. Quietly the most heroic character in the book.
  - **The split:** in the Labyrinth, **the Allocator** (a fate-like figure)
    divides Sal's soul into Lyle and Sunny by relative SSP (80/20). Emotional gut
    punch: the hero is cut in two before being whole.

### Guides & mentors
- **The Friar** — Luca Pacioli, the ancient founding sage of the Accrual World;
  the friar-mathematician who wrote down **the Law** (A = L + E) in the 1400s.
  Gandalf-by-way-of-a-monastery: warm, wry, speaks in proverbs, appears at
  pivotal moments. He insists: *"Every action keeps the Balance, or it does not
  happen at all."*
- **Remy** *(the Rookie)* — first-week junior accountant at Summit; the reader's
  human avatar. Bewildered, earnest, takes notes, makes the mistakes the reader
  would make and learns out loud. Remy does the *real* journal entries. Remy is
  our bridge between myth and mechanics.
- **Iris Quinn** *(the Controller)* — Remy's boss. Sharp, kind, Socratic. Never
  hands Remy an answer; asks the exact question that makes Remy (and the reader)
  figure it out. **Iris's questions are the interview-bank questions.**

### Summit & its people
- **Summit Software & Gear, Inc.** — the flagship enterprise (see NUMBERS.md).
  Outdoor-tech: it sells the **TrailHead Pro** software platform *and* physical
  climbing **gear**. This dual nature is what lets the book teach both ASC 606 and
  inventory.
- **Tess & Hari** — Summit's founders; deposit the original $500,000. They
  represent **Equity** — the residual owners, last in line, biggest dreamers.
- **Atlas Corp** — the big customer who buys Sal's bundle. Reliable, pays upfront.
- **Pinnacle Inc.** — a smaller customer who buys gear on credit and then *ghosts*
  — the face of bad debt / CECL (the receivable that dies).
- **Ranger Coffee Co.** — *cameo & callback to the learn deck.* Summit's longtime
  coffee supplier and the company Summit **acquires** in Chapter 12 (the merger),
  generating goodwill. A friendly wink to readers of the app's existing lessons.

### Forces, guardians & "villains" (mostly strict, rarely evil)
- **Tri** — the three-fold bouncer at the Gate of the Three-Way Match. Checks PO,
  Receiving Report, and Invoice. Refuses anything that doesn't line up three ways.
  Not evil — a loyal guardian against fraud.
- **The 606 Sphinx** *(a.k.a. "the Beast")* — guards the Revenue Labyrinth with
  five riddles (the five steps). Slays anyone who tries to grab revenue too early.
- **Lefty & Righty** — twin scribes in the Great Library: **Debit** (Lefty, from
  *debere*, "to owe," sits on the left) and **Credit** (Righty, from *credere*,
  "to entrust," sits on the right). They bicker constantly but are inseparable;
  every entry needs both, and they must always sum equal.
- **Tally** — the **Trial Balance**: a charming, well-meaning citizen who is an
  *honest liar*. Tally proves the books *balance* but cannot prove they're
  *true*. (Running gag: Tally is very proud and very, very wrong sometimes.)
- **Father Time / the Aging** — the slow curse of **depreciation** that ages
  Rocky the equipment year by year. ("What do accountants suffer from that
  ordinary people don't? Depreciation.")
- **Rocky** — the $100,000 server-rack/equipment purchase; a sturdy, loyal
  workhorse who ages under Father Time and teaches depreciation methods.
- **The Oracle of Unpaid Debts** — foresees which receivables will never be paid
  (CECL). Grim but fair; demands you set aside an **Allowance** *before* the loss
  arrives, because hope is not an accounting method.
- **The Ferryman of the Summary** — **Income Summary**, who appears at the Great
  Cleansing to carry the temporary citizens (revenues, expenses) across to the
  permanent shore (Retained Earnings). A Charon figure; the agent of Sal's
  "death" and rebirth.
- **The Auditor** — the inquisitor of the Crucible (PCAOB). Stern, skeptical,
  incorruptible; trusts nothing, verifies everything. *"Be audit you can be."*
- **The Flattering Mirror** — **Non-GAAP / Adjusted EBITDA**: a vain magic mirror
  on the Public Stage that shows a prettier reflection of the numbers. Not a liar,
  exactly — but it leaves things out.
- **The Referee (Reg G / the SEC)** — forces the Flattering Mirror to always show
  the *real* GAAP reflection at least as prominently as the flattering one.
- **The GAAP** — a literal chasm everyone is forever warned to *mind*. Running
  gag and genuine concept (the rulebook + the gap between cash and truth).

## 6. The emotional arc (why anyone keeps reading)

Sal's want: **to be recognized.** Sal's fear: **to be meaningless** — a number no
one ever sees, lost in the noise.

- **Act I (Ch 1–6): Birth & trials.** Sal is born, learns the Law, survives the
  gates, and is *split in two* by the Allocator — the wound at the heart of the
  story. Lyle gets recognized instantly and struts off; Sunny is shoved into the
  liability dungeon (Deferred Revenue) and must earn her way out, $500 at a time.
- **Act II (Ch 7–12): The grind of becoming real.** Sal's pieces are written down
  (journalized), sorted (ledgers), adjusted, aged, and finally assembled into the
  **Financial Statements** — the first time Sal sees their own reflection as part
  of something whole. Sal learns they're not just a number; they're a *line in a
  story* analysts will read. The Merger shows Sal that even whole companies get
  absorbed into bigger ones — nothing is too big to be consolidated.
- **Act III (Ch 13–15): Death, judgment, immortality.** At the **Great Cleansing**,
  Sal (a temporary) is carried off by the Ferryman — Sal "dies" as a revenue. But
  the twist: Sal's essence is *absorbed into Retained Earnings* (permanent equity)
  and then **printed into the 10-K**, read by the whole world. The **Crucible**
  (audit) certifies Sal was *true*. On the **Public Stage**, Sal's number is read
  aloud on the earnings call. Sal wanted to be recognized; Sal becomes
  *remembered*. And as the clock strikes midnight on the new fiscal year, a new
  transaction is born on the loading dock, and the cycle begins again.

**Theme statement:** *To be recognized is to be remembered.* Recognition (the
accounting rule) and remembrance (the human need) are the same word in two
languages, and the book is the bridge between them.

### Secondary themes (seed these throughout)
- **Balance** — every action has an equal, opposite reaction (A = L + E; debits =
  credits). The universe of accounting is conservation of value.
- **Timing over cash** — the whole Accrual World exists because *when* something
  happens matters more than *when the money moves*. Cash is a tourist; economic
  reality is the resident.
- **Truth vs. flattery** — the trial balance that lies, the cash flow that can't,
  the audit that checks, the mirror that prettifies. The book keeps asking: *is
  this number true, or just balanced?*
- **Transformation & identity** — value never sits still: contract asset →
  receivable → cash; asset → expense; gross → net; temporary → permanent.

## 7. The interview device (how the book secretly preps you)

The interview bank (192 questions, 56 topics, 4 levels) is woven in through **Iris
Quinn**. In each chapter, once the concept has been *lived* in the myth and *done*
in the Office, **Iris asks Remy a question** — and that question is a real
interview question (mapped in `02-COVERAGE-MAP.md`). The narrative then *answers
it in scene*, so the reader absorbs an interview-ready answer without it feeling
like a quiz.

- Keep Iris's questions verbatim-close to the bank's `q_en` where it reads
  naturally as dialogue. (The consolidation pass verifies coverage.)
- Higher-level questions (levels 3–4: deferred taxes, consolidation, DuPont,
  variance analysis) come up later, where the company is more grown-up — the
  difficulty curve of the bank matches the maturity of Summit.
- Optional closing micro-feature per chapter: a tiny **"Iris's Margin Notes"**
  box that lists the 2–4 interview questions that chapter just answered (a study
  aid; consolidation can standardize these).

## 8. Running gags & motifs (sprinkle, don't overdo)

- **"Mind the GAAP."** A literal chasm; said as both safety warning and pun.
- **The introvert/extrovert accountant joke** (Prologue): how to tell them apart —
  the extrovert stares at *your* shoes. Sets the "accounting is secretly fun" tone.
- **"Everybody counts."** The realm's motto, carved over the Genesis gate.
- **The Balance physically tilts.** When an entry is wrong, the world literally
  tips; characters grab the furniture until it's fixed (debits = credits).
- **Father Time and depreciation:** the only thing that ages in this world is
  equipment, and it complains about it.
- **Lefty & Righty bickering** about which side is more important (neither;
  they're equal, always).
- **"Be audit you can be."** The Auditor's grim motto.
- **The Friar's proverbs** close most chapters — short, quotable, true.
- **Cash is a tourist.** Recurring line about cash showing up late and taking all
  the credit while accrual did the work.

## 9. Continuity rules (so 16 parallel writers stay one book)

1. **Names are fixed.** Use exactly the names in §5 and `NUMBERS.md`. Never
   rename Sal, Lyle, Sunny, Remy, Iris, the Friar, Summit, Atlas, Pinnacle,
   Ranger, Rocky, Tri, Tally, the Allocator, the Ferryman, the Oracle.
2. **Numbers come from `NUMBERS.md`.** No freelancing figures.
3. **One-way road.** Respect station order; callbacks yes, retreads no.
4. **Sal is temporary; Lyle/Sunny are facets of Sal.** The split happens *only*
   in Chapter 4 and is permanent after.
5. **Voice is shared** (§2). When in doubt, warmer and clearer beats cleverer.
6. **Each chapter sets up the next and pays off the last** via the
   "callbacks/foreshadowing" fields in its brief (see `00-OUTLINE.md`).
7. **Accuracy beats whimsy, always.** If a metaphor would mislead, fix it.
8. **Don't re-explain a concept already taught** — reference it. Each concept has
   one "home" chapter (the coverage map); other chapters may *use* it but not
   re-teach it from scratch.

## 10. Title & front matter (proposals; consolidation picks final)

- **Working title:** *The Accrual World: How One Transaction Grew Up* — or — *Sal:
  An Accounting Odyssey* — or — *The Ledgend of Sal.* (Final chosen in consolidation.)
- **Subtitle:** *A (surprisingly fun) journey from a handshake to the 10-K.*
- **Epigraph:** the Friar's Law — "Assets = Liabilities + Equity. Everything else
  is commentary."
- **Dedication (joke):** "For everyone who has tried to understand accounting a
  thousand times. This is attempt one thousand and one. It works."
