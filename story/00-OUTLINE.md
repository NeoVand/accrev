## CHAPTER 4 — The Labyrinth of Revenue (and the Three States of Matter)
- **Station:** The Labyrinth of Revenue (the **606 Sphinx**; the **Allocator**).
- **The big one.** Sal is born for real here (the Atlas deal is signed) and is
  split in two. Highest-stakes emotional chapter of Act I.
- **Concepts:** the ASC 606 **five-step model** (identify contract → identify
  performance obligations → determine transaction price → allocate by relative
  SSP → recognize as/when satisfied); revenue recognized on **control transfer**,
  not cash; **point in time vs over time**; **variable consideration** estimated
  up front (so revenue isn't overstated); **principal vs agent** (gross vs net);
  SSP estimation methods when not observable (adjusted market, expected cost plus
  margin, residual). Then the **three states of contractual matter**: **deferred
  revenue** (liability — paid before earned), **contract asset** (earned but right
  to bill is conditional), **accounts receivable** (earned, unconditional). The
  light-switch moment: contract asset → receivable.
- **Interview topics:** `revenue-recognition`, `deferred-revenue`,
  `matching-principle` (intro; home Ch 6), `accrual-vs-deferral` (intro; home Ch 8),
  `transaction-impact` (partial).
- **Learn-deck echo:** Part *timing* — "a question of timing"; "the five-step model."
- **Numbers:** §4 in full — SSP 80,000 / 20,000; bundle 90,000; allocate
  72,000 / 18,000; support $500/mo; 3 months earned in Year 1 → support rev 1,500;
  deferred revenue 16,500 at Dec 31. Principal/agent fork on the support piece.
- **Beats:** (1) Sal is born at the signing, whole and proud, $90,000 of pure
  potential. (2) The Sphinx blocks the exit with five riddles; Sal must answer all
  five to be recognized. (3) Step 4 = the Allocator: Sal is *split* into Lyle
  ($72k) and Sunny ($18k) by relative SSP — the wound. (4) Lyle is recognized
  instantly (control transfers with the download key) and struts off as Somebody.
  (5) Sunny is shoved into the Deferred Revenue dungeon — a *liability* — and must
  earn freedom $500/month. (6) The three states explained via where each character
  stands. (7) Office: Remy books Dr Cash 90,000 / Cr License Rev 72,000 / Cr
  Deferred Rev 18,000, then the monthly $500 unwind; Iris drills principal vs agent.
- **Callbacks:** Ch 2 (the $90,000 price tag, forged in the War Room, is now Sal);
  Ch 1 (control vs cash = accrual). **Foreshadow:** Sunny's 36-month journey
  (recurs through the book); the commission that closed this deal (Ch 6); Sal's
  numbers landing on the income statement (Ch 10); Sal as a temporary who will be
  closed (Ch 13).
- **Iris's Questions:** "Walk me through the five-step revenue model"; "When do you
  recognize revenue at a point in time vs over time?"; "What's deferred revenue and

## CHAPTER 5 — The Hall of Inventory (FIFO vs LIFO)
- **Station:** The Hall of Inventory (a warehouse with a cost-flow conveyor).
- **Concepts:** inventory valuation & the **cost flow assumption** (flow of *costs*
  need not match physical flow); **FIFO** vs **LIFO**; **COGS** and how method
  choice changes COGS, net income, ending inventory, and taxes — *especially under
  inflation*; why FIFO suits perishables and gives a realistic balance sheet; why
  LIFO can minimize taxes when prices rise; gross profit.
- **Interview topics:** `inventory-valuation`, `cogs`.
- **Learn-deck echo:** Part *measurement* — "three flow assumptions"; "same facts,
  three answers."
- **Numbers:** §5 — lots $10 / $12 / $14; sell 2,000 units @ $30 → gear revenue
  60,000; FIFO COGS 22,000, ending inv 14,000; LIFO COGS 26,000, ending inv 10,000;
  AR 60,000 of which 15,000 outstanding. Books use FIFO.
- **Beats:** (1) Gear units line up on the conveyor; the question: when one sells,
  *which cost* leaves with it? (2) FIFO line (oldest cost exits first) vs LIFO line
  (newest cost exits first) — same shelf, two stories. (3) Inflation makes the
  choice matter: LIFO sends the expensive new cost to COGS → lower profit, lower
  tax. (4) The milk vs steel bars analogy (perishable → FIFO). (5) Office: Remy
  computes both, sees net income and taxes change with *nothing physical changing*;
  Iris: "Same warehouse. Two truths. Choose, and disclose."
- **Callbacks:** Ch 2 (the gear cost set in the War Room); Ch 1 (inventory is an
  asset until sold, then becomes an expense — transformation). **Foreshadow:** COGS
  feeds the income statement (Ch 10); the asset-becomes-expense idea recurs in
  matching (Ch 6) and depreciation (Ch 9).
- **Iris's Questions:** "What is FIFO vs LIFO and when would a company use each?";
  "Under inflation, how does the choice affect COGS, net income, and taxes?";
  "What is cost of goods sold and how is it calculated?"
- **Word target:** ~3,600.

## CHAPTER 6 — The Matching Forge (ASC 340-40)
- **Station:** The Matching Forge (where costs are welded to the revenues they earn).
- **Concepts:** the **matching principle** (expenses belong in the period of the
  revenue they helped create); **capitalize vs expense** (is a cost a future
  benefit = asset, or used up = expense?); capitalizing **incremental costs of
  obtaining a contract** under **ASC 340-40** (the sales commission becomes a
  Deferred Contract Cost asset, amortized over the contract); **amortization**
  (spreading an intangible/deferred cost over time); intro to **prepaid expense**
  as the same idea (home Ch 8) and to **PP&E capitalization** (home Ch 9).
- **Interview topics:** `matching-principle`, `capitalize-vs-expense`,
  `amortization`, `prepaid-expense` (intro), `ppe` (capitalization intro).
- **Learn-deck echo:** Part *timing* — "costs follow revenues."
- **Numbers:** §6 — $15,000 commission capitalized; $416.67/month over 36; Year-1
  amortization $1,250; asset remaining $13,750. Cross-reference Sunny's matching
  36-month horizon (Ch 4).
- **Beats:** (1) The salesperson who landed Sal gets a $15,000 commission — should
  Summit expense it all today? (2) The Forge says no: that cost *bought* a
  three-year relationship; it must be matched to those three years. (3) The
  commission is forged into an *asset* (Deferred Contract Cost), then chipped away
  $416.67/month — perfectly in step with Sunny's $500/month support revenue. (4)
  The general rule: capitalize a future benefit, expense a used-up cost (with
  prepaid insurance as a teaser of the same logic). (5) Office: Remy books it;
  Iris: "If you expensed it all today you'd punish today for a gift to the future.

- **Callbacks:** Ch 4 (the deal that earned the commission; Sunny's 36 months).
  **Foreshadow:** prepaid insurance unwinds in Ch 8; PP&E depreciation in Ch 9 is

- **Iris's Questions:** "What is the matching principle?"; "When do you capitalize a

## CHAPTER 9 — The Aging Room & the Oracle of Unpaid Debts
- **Station:** The Aging Room (**Father Time**; **Rocky**) + the Oracle's cave.
- **Concepts:** **depreciation** as systematic allocation of a long-lived asset's
  cost (matching, for big physical things); the four methods — **straight-line,

  how each shapes early net income & tax; **PP&E**, gross cost vs **net book
  value**, **accumulated depreciation** as a contra-asset; then bad debt: the
  **incurred-loss** model's failure (2008) and the shift to **CECL** (expected
  losses over the asset's life, forward-looking); the **allowance for credit
  losses** (a contra-asset → net realizable value); the **write-off** (no income-
  statement impact because the expense was already taken). Contra-accounts fully.
- **Interview topics:** `depreciation`, `ppe`, `bad-debts-allowance`,
  `contra-accounts` (full).
- **Learn-deck echo:** Part *measurement* — "equal expense each year"; "front-load
  the expense"; "expense tied to usage"; "estimate now, write off later."
- **Numbers:** §2 depreciation table (SL 18,000; DDB 40,000; SYD 30,000; units);
  net book value 82,000; §7 allowance 4,000 on AR 15,000 → net AR 11,000.
- **Beats:** (1) Father Time visits Rocky; Rocky ages — the curse of depreciation

## CHAPTER 12 — The Vault of Mergers
- **Station:** The Vault of Mergers (Summit acquires **Ranger Coffee Co.**).
- **Concepts:** **business combinations** (acquisition method; identify acquirer;
  measure assets/liabilities at fair value); **goodwill** (purchase price over fair
  value of net identifiable assets) & **other intangibles** (brand, customer
  lists); **consolidation** (parent + subsidiary into one set of statements;
  eliminating intercompany balances; non-controlling interest); **impairment**
  (goodwill isn't amortized but tested; written down if value drops — depreciation's
  violent cousin); **deferred taxes** arising from fair-value step-ups.
- **Interview topics:** `business-combinations`, `consolidation`,
  `goodwill-intangibles`, `impairment`, `deferred-taxes` (reinforce).
- **Learn-deck echo:** advanced; "extends the deck" (these are level 3–4 topics).
- **Numbers:** a clean acquisition example — Summit pays $X for Ranger; Ranger's
  net identifiable assets fair-valued at $Y; goodwill = X − Y; later an impairment
  write-down. Keep figures illustrative; flag for the Numbers agent. (Ranger ties
  to the learn deck's example company.)
- **Beats:** (1) Summit buys Ranger Coffee — the learn deck's old hero joins the
  story. (2) Fair-value the pieces; the premium paid that *can't* be pinned to any
  identifiable asset becomes **goodwill** — "the price of believing." (3)
  Consolidation: two companies, one set of books; intercompany coffee sales get
  eliminated so Summit can't sell to itself for profit. (4) Goodwill isn't aged
  like Rocky — it's *tested*; when Ranger underperforms, an **impairment** carves
  it down. (5) Office: Remy sees the consolidation worksheet; Iris: "You can buy a
  company. You can't buy back the premium if the dream sours."
- **Callbacks:** Ranger from the learn deck; Ch 9 (impairment vs depreciation);
  Ch 11 (deferred taxes, frameworks). **Foreshadow:** goodwill & the acquisition
  show up in the 10-K notes (Ch 15); the auditor scrutinizes the impairment
  estimate (Ch 14).
- **Iris's Questions:** "What is goodwill and how is it calculated?"; "How is
  goodwill different from other intangibles, and is it amortized?"; "What does
  consolidation do, and what's eliminated?"; "What triggers an impairment?"
- **Word target:** ~4,000.

## CHAPTER 13 — The Great Cleansing (Closing & Reversing Entries)
- **Station:** The Great Cleansing (the **Ferryman of the Summary**; the permanent
  shore = **Retained Earnings**).
- **The emotional climax.** Sal, a temporary citizen, faces "death."
- **Concepts:** **permanent (real) vs temporary (nominal) accounts**; **closing
  entries** (the four-step choreography: close revenues → close expenses → close
  Income Summary to Retained Earnings → close dividends); the **Income Summary** as
  the transient holding account; **Retained Earnings** as where the year's net
  income permanently lands; the **post-closing trial balance** (only permanents
  remain); **reversing entries** (optional, first day of the new year; mirror the
  accruals so the new-year clerk can't double-count — the "time-travel trick" that
  reverses the $18,000 wage accrual).
- **Interview topics:** `closing-entries`.
- **Learn-deck echo:** Part *cycle* — "reset the temps."
- **Numbers:** close all temporaries; net income → Retained Earnings; §7 wage
  accrual reversed Jan 1 (Dr Wages Payable 18,000 / Cr Wage Expense 18,000) so the
  Jan 5 cash payment auto-nets to zero.
- **Beats:** (1) Year-end. The Ferryman comes for the temporaries — revenues and
  expenses, including Sal. (2) Sal realizes they will be *closed* — a revenue
  cannot survive into the new year. The grief beat. (3) But the Ferryman carries
  Sal's net contribution across to the permanent shore: Sal's value is *absorbed
  into Retained Earnings* — Sal doesn't vanish, Sal becomes part of Summit's
  permanent net worth. Rebirth, not death. (4) The post-closing trial balance:
  only the immortals (assets, liabilities, equity) remain; the temporaries are
  zero, ready for a fresh year. (5) Reversing entries: the time-travel trick that
  undoes the wage accrual so the new clerk doesn't book it twice. (6) Office: Remy
  runs the close; Iris explains why retained earnings is the bridge between years.
- **Callbacks:** Ch 8 (the wage accrual now reverses); Ch 1 (temporary vs permanent,
  foreshadowed); Ch 10 (net income → retained earnings, now executed). **Foreshadow:**
  Sal's number, now in Retained Earnings, will be printed in the 10-K (Ch 15); the
  new fiscal year begins the cycle anew (epilogue beat in Ch 15).
- **Iris's Questions:** "Which accounts are temporary vs permanent?"; "Walk me
  through the closing process"; "What is the Income Summary and where does it net
  to?"; "What is a reversing entry and why use one?"
- **Word target:** ~3,800.

## CHAPTER 14 — The Crucible (The Independent Audit)
- **Station:** The Crucible (the **Auditor**; the **PCAOB**).
- **Concepts:** why an audit exists (independent assurance the statements are free
  of material misstatement, fairly presented per GAAP); **substantive tests of
  details** (vouch transactions back to source docs — Sal's PO/receiving report/
  invoice; confirm cash with banks; observe inventory counts) vs **substantive
  analytical procedures** (build an independent expectation — e.g., payroll =
  last year × raise × headcount — and compare); **materiality**; PCAOB **AS 2315**
  (the more persuasive the analytics, the fewer detail tests); **internal controls**
  revisited; **accounting changes & errors / restatements** (if the auditor finds
  a material error, you fix and disclose).
- **Interview topics:** `audit`, `internal-controls` (reinforce),
  `accounting-changes-errors`.
- **Learn-deck echo:** Part *statements* — "the auditor's first check" (trial
  balance) extended to a full audit.
- **Numbers:** the auditor re-vouches Sal's $90,000 to the three-way match docs;
  an analytical expectation on payroll; tests the $4,000 allowance and the goodwill
  impairment estimate for reasonableness.
- **Beats:** (1) Before Summit can go public, the Auditor puts the books in the
  Crucible. (2) Tests of details: the Auditor literally walks Sal's number back to
  the signed contract and the download-key handover — *prove control transferred*.
  (3) Analytical procedures: the Auditor predicts payroll without looking at a
  single timesheet and nails it. (4) The Auditor challenges the soft estimates
  (allowance, impairment) — judgment under scrutiny. (5) A small error is found and
  corrected (intro to restatements). (6) Office: Remy assembles the audit support;
  Iris: "Trust is nice. Evidence is better. *Be audit you can be.*"
- **Callbacks:** Ch 3 (the very documents Tri guarded), Ch 9 (the allowance
  estimate), Ch 12 (impairment estimate), Ch 7 (vouching entries). **Foreshadow:**
  the clean opinion unlocks the Public Stage (Ch 15).
- **Iris's Questions:** "What's the purpose of an audit?"; "Tests of details vs
  analytical procedures — when do you use each?"; "What is materiality?"; "What
  happens when a material error is found in prior statements?"
- **Word target:** ~3,800.

## CHAPTER 15 — The Public Stage (The 10-K, the Earnings Call & Recognition at Last)
- **Station:** The Public Stage (the SEC; the **Hall of Records** = the 10-K; the
  earnings call arena; the **Flattering Mirror**).
- **The finale + epilogue.** Sal achieves immortality; the cycle restarts.
- **Concepts:** **Form 10-K** anatomy (Item 1 Business, Item 1A Risk Factors,
  **Item 7 MD&A** — the narrative soul, **Item 7A** market risk, **Item 8**
  financial statements + **Notes**, incl. the revenue-recognition note where the
  ASC 606 judgments live); **financial reporting** as translating data into a
  public story; the **earnings call** & **Non-GAAP** measures (**Adjusted
  EBITDA**) — the **Flattering Mirror**; **Regulation G** & the **prominence
  requirement** (GAAP shown at least as prominently; every Non-GAAP metric
  reconciled to GAAP); **quality of earnings** (are the earnings real and
  repeatable?). Then the never-ending cycle.
- **Interview topics:** `financial-reporting`, `quality-of-earnings`,
  `gaap-ifrs` (reinforce), `performance-measurement` (reinforce).
- **Learn-deck echo:** Part *capstone* — "what's in a 10-K"; "reading top to
  bottom"; "the footnotes matter"; "every number is a decision."
- **Numbers:** §9 EBITDA bridge (NI 15,000 → Adjusted EBITDA 50,000, illustrative);
  Sal's revenue appears in the income statement and is explained in the rev-rec note.
- **Beats:** (1) The 10-K is filed; Summit goes public as SMMT. Sal's number is
  *printed in the Hall of Records* — the recognition Sal dreamed of, now permanent
  and public. (2) The MD&A: management explains the *why* behind the numbers. (3)
  The earnings call: executives hold up the Flattering Mirror (Adjusted EBITDA);
  the Referee (Reg G) forces the true GAAP reflection beside it. (4) Quality of
  earnings: are these profits real? (5) The audit's clean opinion vouches the whole
  thing. (6) Sal, now a line in the public record *and* a permanent part of
  Retained Earnings, is finally *remembered* — recognition fulfilled, both senses.
  (7) **Epilogue:** midnight on the new fiscal year; a new transaction is born on
  the loading dock; the Friar smiles; the cycle begins again. Callback to the
  Prologue's promise — now the reader can read the truest story a business tells.
- **Callbacks:** the *entire book* — Sal's whole journey; the Prologue's promise;
  Ranger (now consolidated); the Friar's first proverb. **Foreshadow:** the next
  cycle (and, wink, the future interactive version of this very book).
- **Iris's Questions:** "What are the key items in a 10-K?"; "What is Adjusted
  EBITDA and why do companies report it?"; "What does Regulation G require?"; "What
  is 'quality of earnings'?"
- **Word target:** ~4,400 (includes the epilogue beat).
