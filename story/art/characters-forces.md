# Characters — The Forces, Guardians & "Villains"

*Art reference for the gatekeepers, fates, and adversaries of the Accrual World.
Companion to `characters-core.md` (the principal cast). Each entry has (1) a prose
CHARACTER DESCRIPTION for the writers and the art director, and (2) a ready-to-use
IMAGE PROMPT that is **subject-only** — the global **STYLE SUFFIX** from `STYLE.md`
(medium, line/texture, lighting, finish) is appended separately by the workflow.*

**Palette anchor** (woven into every prompt so it reads correctly even before the
suffix lands): warm cream ground **#fbf6ee**, dusty terracotta accent **#b5635a**,
warm gold **#a88a5c**, dark-plum ink **#2a1f2d**, soft cream highlights and a
muted sage **#6b8e62** / clay-red **#a6453f** only where a character earns it.

**Casting note.** These are mostly *strict, not evil* — loyal guardians of the
Balance. The real villain of the book is confusion. Design them as formidable and
characterful, never cruel; even the scariest face should feel *fair*. Keep each
silhouette instantly distinct from the others and from the core cast.

## 1. Tri — the Three-Way-Match Bouncer

**Concept:** source documents & internal controls (Gate of the Three-Way Match, Ch 3).

**CHARACTER DESCRIPTION.** Tri is one being with three faces and three sets of
arms — a single broad-shouldered doorman fused from triplets who never quite
agree. The three heads are stacked and slightly fanned like a hand of cards: the
left face squints at a **Purchase Order** ("what we *wanted*"), the middle face
reads a **Receiving Report** ("what we *got*"), the right face audits a
**Supplier Invoice** ("what we *owe*"). He will not open the gate until all three
faces nod at once — and they are pedantic, so this takes a while. Built like a
nightclub bouncer crossed with a customs officer: a velvet-rope stance, a clipboard
chained to one wrist, an ink-stamp the size of a fist in another hand (it reads
**MATCHED** in terracotta; the rejected stamp reads **HOLD** in clay-red). His
uniform is a deep-plum doorman's coat with three rows of gold buttons and three
breast pockets, each holding one document. A loyal guardian against fraud, not a
thug: stern, fair, faintly bureaucratic, secretly proud of every forgery he's
caught. When the three documents disagree (the invoice says 1,000 racks, the
receiving report says 800), all three faces frown in unison and the gate's iron
portcullis slams — the famous "hard lock."

**Palette:** plum coat, triple rows of gold buttons, terracotta rope and stamp,
cream documents, ink-black portcullis behind.

**IMAGE PROMPT.**
> A formidable nightclub-bouncer guardian standing in a stone gateway, built as a
> single broad figure with **three stacked faces** fanned like a hand of cards —
> the left face squinting at a purchase-order scroll, the middle face reading a
> receiving report, the right face scrutinizing an invoice — and three pairs of
> arms. He wears a deep dark-plum doorman's coat with three vertical rows of warm
> gold buttons and three breast pockets each holding a cream document. One hand
> grips a fist-sized ink stamp reading "MATCHED" in dusty terracotta; a clipboard
> is chained to his wrist; a thick terracotta velvet rope crosses the gate. Behind
> him, a heavy iron portcullis half-lowered. Stern but fair expression on all
> three faces, faintly bureaucratic, proud. Warm cream stone, plum, gold and
> terracotta palette. Full-figure heroic portrait, slightly low angle.

## 2. The 606 Sphinx — the Riddler of the Revenue Labyrinth

**Concept:** ASC 606 five-step revenue model (Labyrinth of Revenue, Ch 4). Also
called "the Beast." Slays anyone who grabs revenue too early.

**CHARACTER DESCRIPTION.** A colossal stone-and-living sphinx coiled across the
only exit of the Revenue Labyrinth — lion's body, folded eagle-wings, and the calm,
ancient face of a being who has heard every excuse. She is not a monster; she is a
*standard*, and she enforces it with terrible patience. Five riddles, five steps:
carved into the stone of her flanks and glowing faintly gold are the **five
performance gates** — *identify the contract, identify the obligations, set the
price, allocate, recognize*. Each paw rests on a worn flagstone numbered 1–5. Her
mane is made of contract clauses and fine print that ripple like fur; her eyes are
two slow-burning gold lamps that read *control*, not cash — she can see the exact
instant a thing is truly *earned*, and nothing fools her. Around her base, the
bleached outlines of transactions that tried to be recognized too soon — a gentle,
non-gory memento, like chalk figures. She is grand, riddling, immovable, and —
when you answer all five correctly — unexpectedly warm, lowering a paw to let you
pass. Scale: she dwarfs Sal; her head alone is the size of a doorway.

**Palette:** weathered cream-sandstone body, terracotta dust on the flanks, gold
glowing glyphs and lamp-eyes, plum shadow in the labyrinth behind.

**IMAGE PROMPT.**
> A colossal ancient sphinx coiled across the exit of a stone labyrinth — a
> lion's body the color of weathered cream sandstone, folded eagle wings, and a
> calm timeless human face. Her mane appears woven from rippling lines of contract
> fine-print. Glowing warm-gold glyphs are carved along her flanks in five
> numbered bands; her two eyes are slow-burning gold lamps. Each enormous paw
> rests on a worn flagstone numbered 1 through 5. Faint chalk-pale outlines of
> small failed figures scattered at her base. Dusty terracotta sand drifts across
> the floor; deep plum shadows recede into the labyrinth behind her. Monumental,
> patient, riddling, faintly benevolent. Wide majestic full-body composition,
> low dramatic angle, tiny human figure for scale at the lower edge.

## 3. The Allocator — the Fate Who Splits the Sale in Two

**Concept:** Step 4, allocate by relative standalone selling price; the moment Sal
is cut into **Lyle ($72k)** and **Sunny ($18k)** at 80/20 (Ch 4). The wound at the
heart of the story.

**CHARACTER DESCRIPTION.** The Allocator is the most unsettling figure in the
book *because* it is the gentlest — a tall, serene, faceless fate in the deep
center of the Labyrinth, robed in cream and gold like a Renaissance allegory of
Justice or the Fates, but with a tailor's calm instead of a god's wrath. It holds
a great pair of **golden shears** and a balance-scale whose two pans are already
weighted **80** and **20**. Where a face should be there is a smooth oval of warm
plum-dark, or a thin gold ratio-line drawn across it. Its hands are precise and
many-fingered, a seamstress's hands; threads of pure light run from a single
glowing whole-soul (Sal, $90,000) and the Allocator divides them — not violently,
but *exactly* — into two skeins: a bright confident gold one (Lyle) and a smaller,
quieter terracotta one (Sunny). The cut is clean, inevitable, almost tender, and
it cannot be undone. It speaks in proportions. It is fate disguised as arithmetic:
beautiful, impersonal, and final. There is grief in the image but no malice — the
Allocator is simply *doing the math the world requires*.

**Palette:** cream-and-gold robes, plum void-face, terracotta-and-gold light
threads, a single bright gold figure and one smaller terracotta figure emerging.

**IMAGE PROMPT.**
> A tall serene faceless fate standing at the heart of a dim stone labyrinth,
> robed in flowing cream and warm gold like a Renaissance allegory of the Fates.
> Where a face should be: a smooth oval of dark plum crossed by a single thin gold
> line. In its precise, many-fingered seamstress hands it holds a large pair of
> golden shears and a small balance scale whose two pans are marked 80 and 20.
> Threads of glowing light flow from one bright whole orb of soul-light and are
> being cleanly divided into two skeins — a larger confident gold strand and a
> smaller quieter terracotta strand — each forming the faint silhouette of a small
> figure. The cut is clean, tender, inevitable. Solemn, beautiful, impersonal.
> Deep plum shadows, cream-gold robes, terracotta accents. Centered vertical
> portrait, soft directional light from above.

## 4. Tally — the Trial Balance, the Honest Liar

**Concept:** the trial balance that proves the books *balance* but cannot prove
they're *true* (Great Library, Ch 7). Running gag: very proud, very, very wrong.

**CHARACTER DESCRIPTION.** Tally is the most lovable nuisance in the realm — a
plump, beaming, slightly officious little person shaped like a perfectly level
**set of balance scales** worn as a body: a round brass-and-gold torso with two
shallow pans for hands, one labeled **Debits**, one labeled **Credits**, held out
proudly and *exactly* even. Tally's whole identity is being balanced, and Tally
announces it constantly — chest puffed, ledger tucked under one arm, a rosette
medal that reads **IT BALANCES!** pinned to the lapel. Round spectacles, a clerk's
visor, ink-stained cuffs, and an expression of total, unearned confidence. The
joke and the lesson live in Tally's body: the two pans are *equal*, so Tally is
delighted — but the coins in each pan might be in entirely the wrong accounts and
Tally would never know. Tally cannot see *truth*, only *symmetry*. When something
is genuinely wrong but still balances, Tally keeps smiling, oblivious, which is
exactly the point. Warm, earnest, a little vain, never malicious — the friend who
is confidently incorrect. Visually small and rounded so they never read as a
threat; the threat is the *false comfort* they offer.

**Palette:** brass-gold scale-body, cream shirt and visor, terracotta rosette and
sash, plum ink-stains, gold "balanced" sparkle.

**IMAGE PROMPT.**
> A plump, beaming, officious little clerk-creature whose body is a perfectly
> level set of balance scales — a round brass-and-gold torso, and two shallow
> hanging pans for hands held out exactly even, one engraved "Debits" and one
> "Credits," each holding a few cream coins. The figure wears a green-tinged
> clerk's visor, round spectacles, ink-stained cuffs, and a bright terracotta
> rosette medal reading "IT BALANCES!" on its lapel, with a ledger tucked under
> one arm. Expression: total, delighted, slightly vain, completely unearned
> confidence. Small and rounded, utterly unthreatening, faintly comic. Warm cream
> background, brass-gold body, terracotta and plum accents, a tiny gold sparkle of
> false pride. Centered charming character portrait, eye-level, soft warm light.

## 5. Father Time — the Aging / Depreciation

**Concept:** depreciation as the slow curse that ages long-lived assets (Aging
Room, Ch 9). *"What do accountants suffer from that ordinary people don't?
Depreciation."*

**CHARACTER DESCRIPTION.** Father Time is the realm's only true force of decay,
but he's a *kindly* one — a tall, stooped, hourglass-headed elder in a dust-grey
and faded-gold robe, moving slowly through the Aging Room laying a gentle,
inevitable hand on everything useful. He is not Death (that's the Ferryman); he is
*wear*. His head or chest holds a great running **hourglass** whose sand is not
sand but fine grey-gold *book-value dust*, draining from a "cost" chamber into an
"accumulated depreciation" chamber below. He carries a slow scythe-shaped pointer
that he uses less to reap than to *tick*, marking each year on the wall. Where his
hand rests, color drains a notch: bright gold to dull gold to grey, year by year,
in even straight-line steps (or front-loaded, where he presses harder). His face
is patient and a little sad, the face of someone who likes the things he must wear
down. Cobwebs of fine ledger-lines trail from his sleeves. He moves at exactly one
speed: the speed of a fiscal year. He is the antagonist Rocky fears and the lesson
the reader learns: usefulness is spent in installments.

**Palette:** dust-grey and faded-gold robe, plum deep-shadow, the hourglass sand a
muted gold draining to grey, faint terracotta on the "cost" chamber.

**IMAGE PROMPT.**
> A tall stooped kindly elder personifying time and wear, robed in dust-grey and
> faded gold, moving slowly through a dim storeroom. In place of his chest or head
> glows a great running hourglass whose falling grains are fine grey-gold dust
> draining from an upper "cost" chamber into a lower "accumulated" chamber. He
> carries a long slender scythe-shaped pointer used for ticking off years, not
> reaping. Where his outstretched hand rests, color visibly drains in even steps
> from bright gold to dull grey. Fine cobweb-thin ledger lines trail from his
> sleeves. His face is patient and faintly sad, fond of the things he must wear
> down. Warm cream-and-plum gloom, muted gold accents, a single softly lit
> hourglass. Atmospheric full-figure portrait, soft side light, slow and solemn
> mood.

## 6. Rocky — the Equipment That Ages

**Concept:** the $100,000 server-rack / equipment purchase; the loyal workhorse
who ages under Father Time and teaches depreciation methods (bought Ch 3, ages Ch 9).

**CHARACTER DESCRIPTION.** Rocky is the heart of the book's "things have feelings
too" warmth — a sturdy, square-shouldered **server rack** brought to life: a tall
steel cabinet of stacked blade-servers with a friendly blinking row of status
lights for eyes, two cable-bundle arms, and stubby caster-wheel feet. He has the
soul of a faithful draft horse or a beloved farm tractor: proud, uncomplaining,
hard-working, a little anxious about getting old. New, he gleams — brushed gold
trim, cream panels, cool terracotta accent stripe, every light a confident green.
As Father Time works on him across the chapters, the gleam dulls, a panel or two
goes grey, his lights flicker amber, his joints (rack-screws) creak — but his
expression stays game and loyal. A small brass plate on his chest reads his
**book value**, ticking down year by year (100,000 → 82,000 → …). He's the
character who makes depreciation *emotional*: the reader watches a good machine
spend its usefulness in installments and root for it anyway. Big, blocky,
huggable-industrial; clearly a beloved appliance, never scary.

**Palette:** brushed-gold trim and cream panels, terracotta accent stripe, green-
to-amber status lights, brass book-value plate; ages toward grey and plum-shadow.

**IMAGE PROMPT.**
> A sturdy, friendly, square-shouldered server-rack brought to life as a loyal
> character — a tall steel cabinet of stacked blade-servers with a horizontal row
> of glowing status-light "eyes," two coiled cable-bundle arms, and small
> caster-wheel feet. Cream-colored side panels, brushed warm-gold trim, a single
> dusty-terracotta accent stripe down the front. The status lights glow confident
> green. A small polished brass nameplate on its chest reads "BOOK VALUE
> $100,000." Its expression is proud, faithful, and game, like a beloved farm
> tractor or draft horse. Big, blocky, huggable-industrial, never menacing. Warm
> cream studio background, gold and terracotta accents, plum soft shadow beneath.
> Full-figure character portrait, gentle three-quarter view, warm even light.

## 7. The Oracle of Unpaid Debts — the CECL Seer

**Concept:** foresees which receivables will never be paid; demands an **Allowance**
set aside *before* the loss arrives (the Oracle's cave, Ch 9). "Hope is not an
accounting method." Pinnacle Inc.'s receivable is the doomed one.

**CHARACTER DESCRIPTION.** The Oracle is grim but fair — a hooded, half-blind
seer in a cave hung with thousands of **fragile paper IOUs** that flutter like
moths, each one a receivable. She is gaunt and still, draped in clay-red and ash,
with one milky prophet's eye that sees the future and one sharp gold eye that reads
the books. Before her sits a shallow scrying basin — not water but pooled ink — in
which she watches each debt's fate play out: most paper IOUs glow warm and steady
(they'll be collected), but some flicker and char at the edges, browning to ash
while still hanging on the line — these are the debts that will *die*, and she can
name them long before they default (Pinnacle's IOU smolders at the back of the
cave). Her demand is the lesson: she will not let you wait for the death. She makes
you set a portion aside *now* — an **Allowance**, shown as a clay-red veil she
drapes over a stack of receivables to mark their *real* collectible value. She is
forward-looking, unsentimental, and never cruel; she simply refuses to let hope do
the accounting. A figure of fate, like the Sphinx and the Allocator, but inward and
quiet rather than monumental.

**Palette:** clay-red and ash robes, one milky-pale eye and one gold eye, cream
fluttering IOUs browning to ash-grey, plum cave dark, a basin of plum-black ink.

**IMAGE PROMPT.**
> A gaunt, still, half-blind seer seated in a shadowy cave whose ceiling is hung
> with thousands of small fragile paper IOUs fluttering like moths. She is draped
> in clay-red and ash-grey robes and a deep hood; one eye is milky prophet-white,
> the other a sharp gold. Before her, a shallow stone basin pooled with plum-black
> ink reflects flickering futures. Most of the hanging paper notes glow warm and
> steady, but several are browning and charring at the edges into ash while still
> on the line — debts fated to die. In one hand she lifts a translucent clay-red
> veil to drape over a small stack of cream notes, marking their true worth.
> Forward-looking, unsentimental, grimly fair, never cruel. Deep plum cave gloom,
> warm cream IOUs, clay-red and gold accents, a single pool of ink-light on her
> face. Atmospheric seated portrait, low candle-warm key light.

## 8. The Ferryman of the Summary — Income Summary, the Charon of Year-End

**Concept:** the **Income Summary** account that, at the Great Cleansing, carries
the temporary citizens (revenues, expenses — including Sal) across to the permanent
shore of **Retained Earnings** (Ch 13). The agent of Sal's "death" and rebirth.

**CHARACTER DESCRIPTION.** The Ferryman is the book's Charon — solemn,
implacable, but *not* malevolent; he is a passage, not an executioner. A tall,
cloaked, faceless figure poling a long flat ledger-boat across the still black
water that separates the **temporary** accounts from the **permanent** shore. His
robe is deep plum fading to black; under the hood, only a faint warm-gold glow
where a face would be — the light of net income carried within. His pole is a long
balanced rule marked with debits and credits; his boat is a single great open
ledger page, and into it climb the year's temporary citizens — small glowing
revenue-figures (Sal among them, terracotta-gold) and expense-figures (cooler,
greyer) — to be *netted* together during the crossing and set down, transformed,
on the far cream-gold shore where **Retained Earnings** waits like a lit harbor.
He appears only at year-end, exactly once, and everyone he carries is changed by
the trip: they do not vanish, they are *absorbed*. There is grief in his arrival
and grace in his purpose. Make him beautiful and quiet — the most cinematic, least
busy image in the set: a lot of dark water, one lantern-figure, one boat, two
shores.

**Palette:** plum-to-black robe and water, a single warm-gold under-hood glow,
small terracotta-gold (revenue) and cool-grey (expense) passengers, a distant
cream-gold shore-light.

**IMAGE PROMPT.**
> A solemn cloaked faceless ferryman, like Charon, standing and poling a long flat
> boat across still black water. The boat is a single enormous open ledger page;
> his pole is a long balanced ruler marked with tiny debit and credit marks. His
> robe is deep plum fading to black; beneath the hood only a faint warm-gold glow
> hints at a face. Climbing aboard are small luminous figures — several warm
> terracotta-gold ones (revenues) and a few cooler grey ones (expenses) — being
> gently netted together. He carries them from a near dark shore of "temporary"
> accounts toward a distant, glowing cream-gold harbor of "permanent" Retained
> Earnings on the far bank. Quiet, cinematic, grieving yet graceful, never
> menacing. Vast dark plum-black water, one warm lantern-glow at center, minimal
> elements. Wide atmospheric composition, deep shadow, single warm light source.

## 9. The Auditor — the Inquisitor of the Crucible (PCAOB)

**Concept:** the independent audit; substantive tests of details vs analytical
procedures; trusts nothing, verifies everything (the Crucible, Ch 14). *"Be audit
you can be."*

**CHARACTER DESCRIPTION.** The Auditor is incorruptible skepticism given a body —
lean, upright, immaculate, the one figure in the realm who will take *nothing* on
faith. Picture a fusion of a forensic detective and a high inquisitor: a precise
slate-grey and plum suit (no warmth, no flourish — warmth could be a bribe), a
green-tinted eyeshade pushed up, and a single great **magnifying lens** worn on a
chain or held to one eye, through which everything is checked against its source.
Pockets and bandolier full of tools of verification: a red **tick-mark pen**, a
bank **confirmation letter**, a ruler, a sample-selection die (chance, not
favoritism). The Crucible around them glows like a forge — every number is heated
and tested for material misstatement, and the Auditor watches, unmoved, for the
ones that warp. Their expression is calm, exacting, and faintly weary, the look of
someone who has heard every story and believes only evidence. Crucially *fair*:
when the books are true, the Auditor signs a clean opinion without ceremony and
without flattery. They are stern, not sinister — the friend of every honest number
and the nightmare of every fudged one. Cooler-toned than anyone else in the book
on purpose: they are the realm's reality check.

**Palette:** slate-grey and plum suit (deliberately cool), gold only on the
magnifying lens and the clean-opinion seal, a clay-red tick-pen, forge-glow
behind.

**IMAGE PROMPT.**
> A lean, upright, immaculate inquisitor-detective embodying incorruptible
> skepticism, standing in a glowing forge-like chamber. Cool slate-grey and plum
> suit with no warmth or ornament; a green-tinted accountant's eyeshade pushed up;
> one eye pressed to a large brass magnifying lens on a chain, examining a document
> against its source. A bandolier and pockets of verification tools — a clay-red
> tick-mark pen, a folded bank-confirmation letter, a ruler, a small sampling die.
> Behind, a crucible-forge where faint glowing numbers are heated and tested,
> some warping. His expression is calm, exacting, faintly weary, utterly fair.
> Deliberately cooler palette than the warm world around it — slate, plum, with
> gold only on the lens and a wax seal. Forge embers in the background. Sharp
> full-figure portrait, hard directional light, high-contrast.

## 10. The Flattering Mirror — Non-GAAP / Adjusted EBITDA

**Concept:** the vain magic mirror on the Public Stage that shows a prettier
reflection of the numbers; not a liar exactly, but it leaves things out (Ch 15).
The **Referee (Reg G / SEC)** forces the true GAAP reflection beside it.

**CHARACTER DESCRIPTION.** The Flattering Mirror is the closest thing to a true
"villain," and even it is more *vain* than evil — a tall, ornate, gilded
full-length **mirror** with a living, smiling face shimmering in its glass,
mounted on the Public Stage in a swirl of gold and rose light. It does not lie
outright; it *adjusts*. Whatever number stands before it, the Mirror shows a
taller, glossier, friendlier version — a modest **$15,000 net income** reflected
back as a gleaming **$50,000 Adjusted EBITDA**, the "ugly bits" (interest, taxes,
depreciation, one-time charges, that awkward stock comp) quietly cropped out of
frame. Its frame is encrusted with the things it loves to remove, rendered as
little carved gargoyles labeled *Interest, Taxes, D&A, "One-Time"*. Its voice is
warm honey; its smile is a salesman's. The honest figure standing before it looks
plain and true; the reflection looks like a magazine cover. The drama of the image
is the **gap** between the figure and its glamorous reflection — and, ideally, a
stern plain referee's hand (Reg G) reaching in to hold up the *real* GAAP
reflection at least as large, right beside the flattering one. Seductive, glossy,
a little too perfect; the lesson is *prefer the plainer mirror*.

**Palette:** lavish gold rococo frame, rose-and-gold flattering glow inside, a
plain true figure in cream-and-plum outside, the honest reflection rendered in
sober plum-grey for contrast.

**IMAGE PROMPT.**
> A tall ornate gilded full-length magic mirror on a theatrical stage, its glass
> holding a living, charming, salesman's-smile face. A plain honest little figure
> labeled "$15,000 NET INCOME" stands before it in cream and plum — but its
> reflection is a glossy, taller, glamorous version labeled "$50,000 ADJUSTED
> EBITDA," bathed in flattering rose-and-gold light, with the unflattering parts
> cropped out of frame. The lavish rococo frame is carved with small gargoyles
> labeled "Interest," "Taxes," "D&A," and "One-Time." A stern plain hand reaches
> in from the side holding up a second, sober plum-grey reflection of the true
> figure, exactly as large. Seductive, glossy, vain, a little too perfect; the
> tension is the gap between the plain figure and its flattering reflection.
> Opulent gold frame, warm rose glow inside, honest cream-plum tones outside.
> Vertical full-length composition, glamorous stage lighting.

## 11. Lefty & Righty — the Debit & Credit Scribes

**Concept:** the twin scribes of the Great Library — **Debit** (Lefty, from
*debere*, "to owe," sits on the left) and **Credit** (Righty, from *credere*, "to
entrust," sits on the right). They bicker constantly but are inseparable; every
entry needs both, and they must always sum equal (Ch 7).

**CHARACTER DESCRIPTION.** Lefty and Righty are mirror-twin clerks who share one
long writing-desk and one unbreakable rule: whatever one writes, the other must
match. They are a comic double-act — the Statler-and-Waldorf, the salt-and-pepper
of the realm — perpetually arguing about who is more important (the answer, which
the Friar keeps repeating, is *neither; you are equal, always*). **Lefty (Debit)**
sits on the left, writes only with the left hand, wears terracotta, and leans
*into* the page — eager, grasping, the one who records what comes *in* and what is
*owed to us*; his ledger column is headed **Dr**. **Righty (Credit)** sits on the
right, writes only with the right hand, wears warm gold, leans *back* with an
arched eyebrow — the one who records what is *entrusted out*, the sources of
things; her column is headed **Cr**. They are physically mirrored: same nib pens
held in opposite hands, same green visors tilted opposite ways, ink-stained on
opposite cuffs, a single shared inkwell in the exact center that they both dip
into. The visual gag: a faint glowing **balance-beam or scale-line** runs across
the desk between them and stays dead level *only* when their two totals match —
when one over-writes, the whole desk (and the room) physically *tilts* until the
other catches up. Inseparable, equal, eternally squabbling, secretly devoted. Keep
them small, warm, and funny — the book's comic heartbeat, not a threat.

**Palette:** Lefty in terracotta, Righty in warm gold, both with cream sleeves and
green visors, plum ink, a level glowing gold balance-line down the desk between them.

**IMAGE PROMPT.**
> Two mirror-twin clerk-scribes sharing one long writing desk, arguing
> good-naturedly. On the left, "Lefty," dressed in dusty terracotta, leans
> eagerly into the page writing with his left hand under a ledger column headed
> "Dr." On the right, "Righty," dressed in warm gold, leans back with an arched
> eyebrow writing with her right hand under a column headed "Cr." They are
> perfectly mirrored: opposite-tilted green visors, ink-stained opposite cuffs,
> matching nib pens in opposite hands, a single shared inkwell exactly between
> them. A faint glowing gold balance-line runs level across the desk between the
> two of them, like a spirit level that only stays even when their totals match.
> Warm, comic, inseparable, equal, eternally bickering. Cream sleeves, terracotta
> and gold clothing, plum ink, warm cream library background. Two-figure
> character portrait, eye-level, lively warm light.
